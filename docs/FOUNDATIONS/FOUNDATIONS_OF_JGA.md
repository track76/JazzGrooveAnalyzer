# Preface

Music is not merely a sequence of sounds.

It is the temporal organization of musical events perceived as a coherent musical performance.

Although every performance generates an acoustic signal that can be physically measured, musical time is not explicitly contained within that signal. Rather, it emerges from the interaction among performers, from the relationships between musical events, and from the temporal organization perceived by the listener.

The Jazz Groove Analyzer (JGA) is founded on the assumption that musical time cannot be directly observed. It must instead be progressively reconstructed through successive levels of abstraction derived from the acoustic signal.

This document establishes the theoretical foundations of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

It intentionally avoids implementation details, mathematical formulations, Digital Signal Processing techniques, and software architecture. Its purpose is to define the conceptual framework from which every future mathematical model, DSP algorithm, software component, and implementation of the JGA will be derived.

---

# 1. Purpose

The Jazz Groove Analyzer is a theoretical and computational framework designed to reconstruct the temporal organization of a musical performance exclusively from its acoustic signal.

Unlike traditional beat tracking systems, the JGA does not assume that musical time is directly observable. Instead, it models musical time as an emergent property progressively reconstructed through successive stages of interpretation.

The primary objective of the JGA is therefore not the detection of beats, but the construction of a coherent temporal representation of an ensemble performance.

This document defines the conceptual foundations of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

# 3. The Nature of Musical Time

Musical time is not directly observable.

What can be directly observed is the acoustic signal generated by a musical performance.

Every representation of musical time results from an interpretative process applied to that signal.

Consequently, the Jazz Groove Analyzer considers musical time not as an intrinsic property of the audio signal, but as a progressively reconstructed representation emerging from successive levels of abstraction.

This distinction between physical observation and musical interpretation constitutes one of the fundamental principles of the JGA.

# 4. The Temporal Abstraction Hierarchy

## 4.1 Overview

The Jazz Groove Analyzer reconstructs musical time through a sequence of progressively more meaningful temporal representations.

Each representation is derived from the previous one while introducing a higher level of semantic interpretation.

The complete hierarchy is defined as follows.

```
Audio Signal
        │
        ▼
Transient
        │
        ▼
Onset
        │
        ▼
PulseCandidate
        │
        ▼
Elementary Metric Event (EME)
        │
        ▼
Metric Cluster (MC)
        │
        ▼
Pulse
        │
        ▼
Internal Metric Timeline (IMT)
```

This hierarchy does not describe a computational pipeline.

Instead, it describes the conceptual process through which the JGA progressively reconstructs the temporal organization of a musical performance.

Each level answers a different theoretical question while introducing a higher level of semantic abstraction.

---

## 4.2 Progressive Abstraction

The objective of each level is not simply to transform data.

Its objective is to reduce ambiguity while increasing the semantic meaning of the temporal representation.

As the hierarchy progresses:

- the amount of ambiguity decreases;
- the amount of musical information increases;
- the representation becomes progressively more suitable for temporal analysis.

Consequently, higher levels do not replace lower ones.

Each level preserves the information generated by the previous levels while adding a new interpretative layer.

---

## 4.3 Observation and Representation

The Temporal Abstraction Hierarchy naturally separates two conceptual domains.

### Observation

The observation domain contains representations directly derived from the acoustic signal.

Its purpose is to observe temporal phenomena without assigning musical meaning.

This domain includes:

- Audio Signal
- Transient
- Onset
- PulseCandidate

---

### Representation

The representation domain contains entities belonging to the theoretical model of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Its purpose is to organize observed temporal phenomena into a coherent representation of musical time.

This domain includes:

- Elementary Metric Event (EME)
- Metric Cluster (MC)
- Pulse
- Internal Metric Timeline (IMT)

The transition between these two domains represents the point at which acoustic observations become part of the temporal model adopted by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

---

## 4.4 Fundamental Property

The Temporal Abstraction Hierarchy is based on the following principle.

> Every level of the hierarchy introduces a new semantic interpretation of temporal information without altering the validity of the previous representations.

This principle ensures that the Jazz Groove Analyzer progressively constructs knowledge rather than simply processing data.

# 5. The Audio Signal

## 5.1 Definition

The Audio Signal is the continuous physical manifestation of the acoustic energy generated during a musical performance.

Within the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the Audio Signal represents the only primary source of information from which every subsequent temporal representation is derived.

No external musical information is assumed.

The JGA does not rely on musical scores, MIDI data, tempo annotations, click tracks, or manually supplied temporal references.

Every element of the temporal model originates exclusively from the observed acoustic signal.

---

## 5.2 The Role of the Audio Signal

The Audio Signal does not explicitly contain a formal representation of beat, tempo, rhythm, or pulse.

These concepts emerge only through successive stages of temporal abstraction.

Instead, the Audio Signal contains the physical phenomena produced by the interaction between musical instruments, performers, and the acoustic environment.

Consequently, every musical concept handled by the Jazz Groove Analyzer must be progressively reconstructed through interpretation.

---

## 5.3 Observation Without Interpretation

The Audio Signal belongs entirely to the observation domain.

At this level, the system performs no musical interpretation.

The signal is treated exclusively as a physical phenomenon that can be observed, measured, and analysed.

Any musical meaning assigned to the signal belongs to higher abstraction levels.

---

## 5.4 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the Audio Signal possesses the following properties.

- It is continuous in the physical domain.
- It is digitally sampled during acquisition.
- It is directly observable.
- It is independent of musical interpretation.
- It constitutes the origin of every subsequent temporal representation.

---

## 5.5 Consequences

Since the Audio Signal contains no explicit representation of musical time, every temporal representation produced by the Jazz Groove Analyzer is necessarily the result of progressive interpretation.

This principle establishes the conceptual separation between the physical domain and the temporal model that underlies the entire theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

# 6. Transient

## 6.1 Definition

A Transient is a rapid local variation of the acoustic signal, typically associated with a sudden change in amplitude, spectral content, or both.

Within the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the concept of Transient is adopted according to its established meaning in acoustics and digital signal processing.

No alternative definition is introduced.

---

## 6.2 The Role of the Transient

The Transient represents the earliest observable indication that a significant acoustic change has occurred.

It provides evidence that the physical properties of the signal have changed over a short temporal interval.

However, a Transient carries no intrinsic musical meaning.

Its existence does not imply the beginning of a musical event, nor does it indicate rhythmic or metric relevance.

---

## 6.3 Observation Domain

The Transient belongs entirely to the observation domain.

Its identification depends exclusively on the physical characteristics of the acoustic signal.

No musical interpretation is involved at this level.

---

## 6.4 Fundamental Properties

Within the JGA theoretical framework, a Transient possesses the following properties.

- It is directly observable from the audio signal.
- It represents a physical phenomenon.
- It may be associated with the beginning of a sound.
- It may also correspond to any abrupt acoustic variation.
- It does not possess intrinsic rhythmic or metric significance.

---

## 6.5 Consequences

A musical performance may contain numerous transient events that are unrelated to the temporal organization of the performance.

Consequently, the presence of a Transient alone is insufficient for constructing any representation of musical time.

Higher levels of abstraction are therefore required before temporal interpretation becomes possible.

# 7. Onset

## 7.1 Definition

An Onset is the perceived beginning of a musical sound or acoustic event.

Within the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the concept of Onset is adopted according to its established meaning in Music Information Retrieval (MIR) and auditory perception.

The JGA does not redefine the concept of Onset.

---

## 7.2 The Role of the Onset

The Onset represents the earliest perceptually meaningful temporal reference associated with a sound event.

Unlike the Transient, which describes a physical variation of the signal, the Onset represents the perceptual beginning of a sound.

Consequently, the Onset constitutes the conceptual transition between physical observation and auditory perception.

The exact temporal position of an Onset may depend on the perceptual characteristics of the sound itself. Consequently, the Onset is not purely a physical property of the acoustic signal but also a perceptual construct.

---

## 7.3 Observation Domain

The Onset remains within the observation domain.

Although it is perceptually defined, it is still derived directly from the acoustic signal and does not yet imply any musical interpretation.

At this level, the system answers only one question:

> When does a sound begin?

No assumptions regarding rhythm, pulse, tempo, or meter are introduced.

---

## 7.4 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, an Onset possesses the following properties.

- It represents the perceptual beginning of a sound.
- It is derived from the acoustic signal.
- It may be associated with one or more transient phenomena.
- It does not possess intrinsic metric meaning.
- It does not necessarily contribute to the musical pulse.

---

## 7.5 Consequences

A musical performance may contain numerous onset events that play no role in the temporal organization of the ensemble.

Therefore, the identification of Onsets alone is insufficient to reconstruct musical time.

The interpretation of metric relevance belongs to higher abstraction levels introduced by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

# 8. The PulseCandidate Concept

## 8.1 Introduction

The PulseCandidate is the first theoretical entity introduced exclusively by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Unlike the concepts of Audio Signal, Transient, and Onset, which are established in acoustics and Music Information Retrieval (MIR), the PulseCandidate belongs entirely to the theoretical framework of the JGA.

Its introduction marks the transition from the observation of acoustic phenomena to the observation of potential temporal structures.

---

## 8.2 Definition

A PulseCandidate is an observed temporal event that may contribute to the temporal organization of a musical performance.

A PulseCandidate does not represent the musical pulse itself.

Instead, it represents an observation that may contribute to the reconstruction of the pulse.

---

## 8.3 The Role of the PulseCandidate

The purpose of the PulseCandidate is not to identify musical time.

Its purpose is to identify observations that may contribute to the temporal organization of the performance.

Consequently, the PulseCandidate belongs to the observation domain of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

It represents the highest level of observation before the construction of the temporal model begins.

---

## 8.4 Observation Without Interpretation

The PulseCandidate does not assign musical meaning.

It does not determine tempo.

It does not determine beat.

It does not determine meter.

It simply identifies observations that may become relevant within the temporal model.

The interpretation of those observations belongs to the subsequent abstraction level.

---

## 8.5 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, a PulseCandidate possesses the following properties.

- It is derived from one or more Onsets.
- It represents an observed temporal event.
- It may contribute to the temporal organization of the performance.
- It does not constitute a musical pulse.
- It does not belong to the temporal model.
- It belongs exclusively to the observation domain.

---

## 8.6 Consequences

Multiple PulseCandidates may be observed during a musical performance without all of them becoming part of the temporal model.

The PulseCandidate therefore represents a necessary intermediate abstraction between auditory observation and metric representation.

Its introduction allows the Jazz Groove Analyzer to separate the observation of temporal evidence from the representation of musical time.

# 9. The Elementary Metric Event (EME)

## 9.1 Introduction

The Elementary Metric Event (EME) is the first entity belonging to the temporal model of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Unlike the PulseCandidate, which belongs to the observation domain, the EME belongs to the representation domain.

Its introduction marks the beginning of the theoretical reconstruction of musical time.

---

## 9.2 Definition

An Elementary Metric Event is the formal metric representation of a PulseCandidate interpreted within the temporal model of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

The EME does not represent an acoustic event.

It represents a metric entity belonging to the temporal model.

---

## 9.3 The Role of the EME

The purpose of the EME is to establish a stable representation of a metrically meaningful temporal event.

Unlike the PulseCandidate, which represents an observation, the EME represents an element of the theoretical model from which higher temporal structures can be constructed.

---

## 9.4 Contextual Interpretation

No PulseCandidate possesses metric meaning in isolation.

Metric meaning emerges exclusively through interpretation within the temporal model reconstructed by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

The EME therefore represents the formal contextualization of a PulseCandidate within that temporal model rather than its direct transformation.

---

## 9.5 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, an EME possesses the following properties.

- It belongs to the representation domain.
- It originates from a PulseCandidate.
- It represents a metrically meaningful event.
- It is independent of the physical signal.
- It constitutes the elementary building block of the temporal model.

---

## 9.6 Consequences

The introduction of the EME establishes the conceptual separation between temporal observation and temporal representation.

From this level onward, the Jazz Groove Analyzer no longer models acoustic observations.

It models musical time.

# 10. The Metric Cluster

## 10.1 Introduction

The Metric Cluster (MC) is the first collective representation introduced by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

While the Elementary Metric Event represents an individual metrically meaningful event, the Metric Cluster represents the temporal relationship among multiple Elementary Metric Events.

Its introduction marks the transition from individual temporal representation to collective temporal organization.

---

## 10.2 Definition

A Metric Cluster is an organized set of Elementary Metric Events that collectively describe a single temporal reference within a musical performance.

The Metric Cluster does not represent a collection of unrelated events.

Instead, it represents the convergence of multiple Elementary Metric Events into a common temporal structure.

---

## 10.3 The Role of the Metric Cluster

The purpose of the Metric Cluster is to organize Elementary Metric Events according to their shared temporal significance.

In doing so, the Metric Cluster establishes the first collective representation of musical time within the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Individual metric events are no longer considered independently.

Instead, they become part of a common temporal organization.

---

## 10.4 Collective Representation

The Metric Cluster introduces a fundamental conceptual shift.

Previous abstraction levels describe individual observations or individual metric entities.

The Metric Cluster is the first entity that represents the collective temporal behaviour of the ensemble.

Consequently, musical time begins to emerge not from isolated events but from their temporal relationships.

---

## 10.5 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, a Metric Cluster possesses the following properties.

- It belongs to the representation domain.
- It is composed of Elementary Metric Events.
- It represents a collective temporal structure.
- It establishes a common temporal reference.
- It serves as the foundation for pulse reconstruction.

---

## 10.6 Consequences

The Metric Cluster represents the first abstraction level at which the Jazz Groove Analyzer models the temporal behaviour of the ensemble rather than individual musical events.

For this reason, the Metric Cluster constitutes the conceptual bridge between metric representation and pulse reconstruction.

# 11. Pulse

## 11.1 Introduction

Within the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the Pulse is not considered a directly observable property of the acoustic signal.

Instead, it emerges from the collective organization of Elementary Metric Events represented by one or more Metric Clusters.

The Pulse therefore belongs entirely to the representation domain.

---

## 11.2 Definition

A Pulse is a coherent temporal reference reconstructed from one or more Metric Clusters.

It represents the fundamental temporal unit used by the Jazz Groove Analyzer to describe the organization of musical time.

---

## 11.3 The Role of the Pulse

The purpose of the Pulse is to establish a stable temporal reference for the analysis of a musical performance.

Unlike the Metric Cluster, which represents the collective organization of metric events, the Pulse represents the temporal reference that emerges from that organization.

---

## 11.4 Emergence

The Pulse is an emergent entity.

It is not detected.

It is not directly observed.

It emerges from the temporal relationships represented by the Metric Clusters.

Consequently, the Pulse constitutes the first explicit representation of musical time within the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

---

## 11.5 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the Pulse possesses the following properties.

- It belongs to the representation domain.
- It emerges from one or more Metric Clusters.
- It is not directly observable.
- It represents a coherent temporal reference.
- It serves as the basis for modelling the temporal evolution of a musical performance.

---

## 11.6 Consequences

The Pulse is not a property of the acoustic signal.

It is a property of the temporal model reconstructed by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Its existence depends entirely on the interpretation and organization of the observed temporal evidence.

# 12. Internal Metric Timeline (IMT)

## 12.1 Introduction

The Internal Metric Timeline (IMT) represents the highest level of temporal abstraction within the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Unlike all preceding entities, which describe observations, metric events, or reconstructed temporal references, the IMT models the continuous temporal behaviour of the musical performance.

It constitutes the final theoretical representation produced by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

---

## 12.2 Definition

The Internal Metric Timeline is the continuous temporal model reconstructed from the sequence of Pulses generated throughout a musical performance.

It represents the internal temporal organization of the ensemble as inferred by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

The IMT does not describe isolated events.

It describes the continuous evolution of musical time.

---

## 12.3 The Role of the IMT

The purpose of the Internal Metric Timeline is to provide a coherent representation of musical time throughout the entire performance.

Rather than representing individual temporal references, the IMT models their evolution, continuity, and internal consistency.

The Internal Metric Timeline represents the internal temporal behaviour of the ensemble rather than an externally imposed temporal grid.

It therefore constitutes the theoretical foundation for every subsequent temporal analysis performed by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

---

## 12.4 Continuous Temporal Representation

Unlike previous abstraction levels, which describe discrete entities, the IMT represents a continuous temporal structure.

This continuity allows the Jazz Groove Analyzer to analyse not only isolated temporal events but also the evolution of musical time throughout the performance.

Consequently, the IMT represents the complete temporal model reconstructed by the system.

---

## 12.5 Fundamental Properties

Within the theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer, the Internal Metric Timeline possesses the following properties.

- It belongs to the representation domain.
- It is reconstructed from the sequence of Pulses.
- It represents the complete temporal organization of the ensemble.
- It models the continuity of musical time.
- It constitutes the highest abstraction level of the temporal model.

---

## 12.6 Consequences

The Internal Metric Timeline is not an observable property of the acoustic signal.

It is the final theoretical representation produced by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

The Internal Metric Timeline provides the temporal reference against which every subsequent analysis of tempo, timing, microtiming, rhythmic stability, and ensemble interaction is performed.

# Representational Fidelity Principle

The Jazz Groove Analyzer is an observational and representational scientific framework.

Every transformation performed by the Jazz Groove Analyzer produces a higher-level representation of the observed musical behaviour without altering the underlying observations.

Consequently, every transformation defined within the JGA shall preserve the observed phenomenon.

The Jazz Groove Analyzer shall never:

- modify physical timestamps;
- realign musical events;
- quantize temporal behaviour;
- smooth temporal observations;
- create artificial musical events;
- alter the observable acoustic signal.

Every transformation changes only the level of representation while preserving the observed musical behaviour.

The Jazz Groove Analyzer performs analysis rather than correction.

A useful analogy is medical imaging.

The JGA performs a temporal CT scan of the ensemble.

It reconstructs a faithful representation of the musical behaviour without modifying the phenomenon being observed.

This principle governs every mathematical transformation, software component and architectural decision throughout the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

# 13. Fundamental Principles and Axioms

The theoretical framework of the Jazz Groove Analyzer is founded upon a small set of fundamental axioms.

These axioms define the conceptual identity of the JGA and govern every mathematical formulation, Digital Signal Processing algorithm, software architecture, and implementation developed within the project.

Any future extension of the Jazz Groove Analyzer shall remain fully consistent with these principles.

---

## Axiom 1 — Acoustic Primacy

The acoustic signal constitutes the only primary source of information available to the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

No external temporal reference, musical score, click track, tempo annotation, MIDI information, or manually supplied musical knowledge is assumed.

Every temporal representation reconstructed by the JGA shall ultimately originate from the observed acoustic signal.

---

## Axiom 2 — Observation Precedes Representation

Observation always precedes representation.

Every entity belonging to the representation domain shall originate from one or more entities belonging to the observation domain.

No temporal representation may exist without prior observation.

The conceptual transition between these two domains occurs between the PulseCandidate and the Elementary Metric Event.

---

## Axiom 3 — Progressive Abstraction

Musical time cannot be directly observed.

It is progressively reconstructed through successive levels of temporal abstraction.

Each abstraction level introduces additional semantic meaning while remaining fully consistent with all preceding levels.

---

## Axiom 4 — Contextual Metric Meaning

No observed temporal event possesses intrinsic metric meaning.

Metric meaning emerges exclusively through interpretation within the temporal model reconstructed by the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Consequently, the same observed event may assume different metric meanings depending on its musical context.

---

## Axiom 5 — Emergent Musical Time

Musical time is not explicitly contained within the acoustic signal.

It emerges from the progressive organization of temporal observations into increasingly coherent metric representations.

The Pulse and the Internal Metric Timeline are therefore emergent entities rather than directly observable phenomena.

---

## Axiom 6 — Internal Consistency

Every abstraction level shall remain logically consistent with all preceding abstraction levels.

Higher-level representations may introduce new semantic information but shall never contradict the observations from which they originate.

The theoretical coherence of the Jazz Groove Analyzer depends upon preserving this consistency throughout the entire temporal reconstruction process.

---

## Consequences

These axioms define the theoretical identity of the Jazz Groove Analyzer.

Every mathematical model, Digital Signal Processing algorithm, software component, validation procedure, and implementation shall be considered valid only if fully consistent with the principles established in this document.
